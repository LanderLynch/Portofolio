Client Work / Transit Card Series

Turning a Daily Commute Into a Collectible Honkai: Star Rail Moment

For this e-money transit series, my goal was to merge the visual energy of Honkai: Star Rail with the compact, everyday function of a public transportation card. I designed the system to feel collectible without losing the practical clarity needed for a real transit product people would carry, tap, and use every day.

Section 1

Project Overview

I developed this project as a custom e-money card series for public transportation, built around the appeal of Honkai: Star Rail characters and the ritual of daily commuting. My objective was to make the card feel like more than a payment tool. I wanted it to behave like a small piece of character merchandise that still fits naturally into real-world transit use.

1

I decided to treat the card as a bridge between fan culture and product utility, so the design had to feel collectible and practical at the same time.

2

My goal was to carry the atmosphere of the game into a format people could use in a daily commute without the composition feeling overworked.

3

The final result frames transit merchandise as part of a lifestyle system, not just a novelty print on plastic.

Section 2

Character Adaptation & Layout

I approached the layout phase by translating high-energy game visuals into the narrow proportions of a standard transit card. The artwork had to hold the identity of the featured characters while still leaving space for the physical smart chip area, transit authority placement, and the visual breathing room needed for a clean final product.

That meant I could not simply drop full character illustrations onto the card. I had to crop, scale, and reposition the imagery so the character presence still felt dramatic, but the layout stayed readable and production-ready. I also used UI-inspired accents from the game to reinforce the Honkai: Star Rail feeling without making the card too crowded for an everyday object.

Front Layout Adaptation

I organized the front side around character impact first, then carefully balanced the empty zones needed for the smart chip and supporting transport marks. The goal was to keep the card expressive without sacrificing functionality.

Why the Layout Needed Restraint

I wanted the card to feel vibrant, but transit cards have almost no tolerance for clutter. Keeping selective negative space was important because the hardware elements and service branding still needed a clear visual home.

Visual Translation Strategy

Rather than replicating a full game screen, I distilled the artwork into a tighter composition. That helped the final design retain the tone of the source material while still looking like a professionally manufactured object.

Section 3

Technical Setup & Print Preparation

I set up the production files between Adobe Illustrator and Adobe Photoshop so the card series would be ready for physical output, not just screen presentation. Illustrator handled the core vector layout and precise placement logic, while Photoshop helped me refine artwork treatment, tonal balance, and mockup presentation.

For print readiness, I configured CMYK-safe color decisions, added proper bleed space, and kept the exported assets high resolution so the details would remain crisp on the plastic card surface. That production discipline mattered because transit card merchandise becomes very unforgiving once it moves from screen to print.

Illustrator Setup

I used Illustrator to control card dimensions, alignment, vector sharpness, and the placement of mandatory production zones. That gave the layout a reliable technical foundation before any mockup styling was introduced.

Photoshop & Output Prep

I used Photoshop to refine the final presentation, test material feel, and make sure colors stayed cohesive once translated toward print conditions. Bleed, resolution, and export discipline were all part of making the design production-aware.
